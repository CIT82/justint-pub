My First Time Cruising

Anticipation & Planning

For years I had dismissed cruising as a vacation style that wasn’t really “me.” I imagined shuffleboard on deck, fixed seating dinners with strangers, and feeling trapped on a floating hotel with no escape. But curiosity has a way of chipping away at old perceptions, and when a friend suggested a three-day Carnival cruise out of Long Beach, California, I found myself clicking through itineraries.

The photos were seductive: gleaming white ships, smiling couples sipping cocktails under umbrellas, families laughing by the pool. The price was even more surprising—less than what I’d spend on a weekend in Las Vegas. Add in the promise of waking up somewhere entirely new, and the idea went from outlandish to irresistible.

Still, I was nervous. What would it feel like to be at sea for the first time? Would I get seasick? Would the ship feel claustrophobic? And perhaps most importantly: what if I hated it?

I spent the weeks leading up to departure oscillating between excitement and anxiety. I pored over packing lists in online forums, debated whether I really needed a formal outfit for “elegant night,” and studied deck plans like they were treasure maps. I downloaded the Carnival Hub app, set up my profile photo, and began counting down the days.

When embarkation day finally arrived, I found myself wheeling a modest suitcase across the smooth concrete of the Long Beach Cruise Terminal. Ahead of me loomed the Carnival Radiance, her signature red, white, and blue funnel towering like a beacon of adventure. My heart raced. This was it—the beginning of my first voyage at sea.

Embarkation Day: Crossing the Threshold

The process of boarding felt strangely ceremonial. Luggage tags affixed, boarding pass in hand, I joined a snaking line of fellow passengers. Excitement buzzed in the air like static. Some families wore matching T-shirts announcing a reunion; others clutched balloon bouquets for birthdays. Everyone seemed to be smiling.

Security and check-in moved quickly, and before I knew it, I was stepping across the gangway. A blast of cool air hit my face as I entered the atrium, and my jaw nearly dropped.

The space was vast, shimmering with light. Glass elevators glided silently up and down, neon accents glowed from above, and the sound of laughter spilled from the nearby bar. It was like stepping into a bustling hotel lobby—except this hotel would soon be gliding into the Pacific Ocean.

I was immediately directed to my stateroom, a cozy inside cabin on Deck 6. Compact yet cleverly designed, it held a queen bed, a small desk, a flat-screen TV, and just enough closet space. It wasn’t luxurious, but it was comfortable, and I found myself oddly charmed by its snugness. This would be my little cocoon at sea.

But there was no time to linger. Muster drill—essential safety orientation—was next. Gathered with hundreds of other passengers, I listened as crew members demonstrated life jackets and explained evacuation procedures. It was sobering yet reassuring.

Once the drill ended, the mood shifted instantly. The ship’s horn bellowed, a deep, resonant sound that vibrated in my chest. People cheered. The deck erupted into a sail-away party. Music blasted, bartenders mixed colorful drinks, and passengers lined the railings, waving as Long Beach shrank behind us. The skyline faded, the Queen Mary slid past, and suddenly, the vast expanse of the Pacific opened before us.

Standing at the rail, the salty wind in my face, I felt a rush of exhilaration. For the first time in my life, I was truly at sea.

Exploring the Ship: Food, Entertainment, and the Floating City

Carnival ships are designed like playgrounds, and the Radiance was no exception. My first task was simply to get my bearings. Deck maps became my bible as I navigated the labyrinth of corridors, staircases, and lounges.

Food, I quickly learned, was at the heart of the experience. The buffet stretched endlessly: carving stations, pasta bars, salad mountains, dessert tables. At Guy’s Burger Joint, sizzling patties topped with melty cheese and piles of fries disappeared almost faster than they could be cooked. BlueIguana Cantina served up tacos and burritos stuffed to the brim. The sheer abundance was dizzying.

Dinner in the main dining room was a more elegant affair. Waiters in crisp uniforms whisked plates of lobster tail, prime rib, and molten chocolate cake to tables. I had worried about awkward silences with strangers, but my assigned tablemates—a couple from Arizona and a family from Portland—were delightful. By the second night, we were swapping stories like old friends.

Entertainment was equally plentiful. I wandered into the theater one evening to find a high-energy Broadway-style revue, complete with sequined costumes and soaring vocals. Later, I ducked into a comedy club where the laughter came fast and loud. At midnight, I found myself on deck, watching a dive-in movie under the stars, bundled in a towel as the ship gently rocked beneath us.

Even downtime had its charms. I lounged by the pool with a frozen drink, browsed the onboard shops, and people-watched in the atrium. Everywhere I turned, there was music, motion, life.

The ship truly was a floating city, and I was eager to explore every corner.

First Port of Call: Ensenada

On the second morning, we docked in Ensenada, Mexico. From my windowless cabin, I couldn’t see the port, but the announcement over the loudspeaker stirred me awake. Excursions beckoned: wine country tours, horseback rides on the beach, ATV adventures.

I chose something simpler: wandering the town. After disembarking, I strolled along the waterfront, where vendors sold colorful blankets, silver jewelry, and sizzling street tacos. The smell of grilled carne asada mixed with the briny sea air, and mariachi music spilled from cantinas.

I sampled fish tacos from a stand that had been serving them for decades, crispy and tender all at once. I sipped a cold cerveza, feeling the sun warm my shoulders. And I chatted with shopkeepers who welcomed cruise passengers with practiced ease.

Though the visit was brief—just a few hours—the flavor of Ensenada lingered. It was a reminder that cruising wasn’t just about the ship. It was also about the places it carried me, the small windows into lives and cultures beyond my own.

A Day at Sea

Sea days, I discovered, are the true essence of cruising. With no port to rush off to, the ship itself becomes the destination.

I began my morning with coffee and a croissant at the café, watching the horizon glow pink as the sun rose. Later, I tried trivia in the lounge, cheered at a hairy-chest contest by the pool, and indulged in a decadent brunch complete with bottomless mimosas.

The rhythm was unhurried. Walk a few steps, and there was something to do—or nothing at all, if that’s what I wanted. I spent an hour reading on the Serenity Deck, Carnival’s adults-only retreat, lulled by the sound of waves. Then I tried the water slide, shrieking like a kid as I splashed into the pool.

That evening, elegant night transformed the ship. Passengers donned cocktail dresses and suits, photographers snapped portraits, and the dining room shimmered with energy. I raised a glass of champagne and felt, for the first time in years, completely unburdened.

The Social Side of Cruising

One of the biggest surprises was how social cruising could be. Strangers became companions quickly: over dinner, in line for coffee, or sitting side-by-side at a show. The shared experience created an instant bond.

I struck up conversations with retirees on their tenth voyage, first-time cruisers like me, and crew members from every corner of the globe. Each story enriched my own. By the end of the trip, I had exchanged emails with people I never would have met otherwise.

Cruising, I realized, wasn’t just about places. It was about people.

Missteps & Surprises

Of course, not everything went smoothly. I underestimated the power of the sun and earned a spectacular sunburn on day one. I got lost more than once in the ship’s winding hallways. I even accidentally ordered two entrees at dinner, not realizing that on a cruise, that was perfectly acceptable.

But these small missteps became part of the charm. They reminded me that firsts are rarely flawless—and that sometimes, mistakes make the best stories.

Final Day & Disembarkation

On the final morning, I stood on deck watching the California coastline reappear. The adventure was ending, yet I didn’t feel sadness. Instead, I felt gratitude. I had tested something new, stepped outside my comfort zone, and found joy in the unfamiliar.

Disembarkation was orderly and efficient. Within an hour, I was back on land, suitcase in hand, already scrolling through future cruise itineraries on my phone.

Lasting Impressions

Looking back, that first Carnival cruise was more than just a vacation. It was a doorway. It showed me that adventure doesn’t always require a plane ticket halfway across the world. Sometimes, it’s waiting at a port just a few hours from home.

I learned that the sea has a rhythm all its own, one that encourages both play and rest. I discovered that sharing meals with strangers can be unexpectedly delightful. And I realized that stepping onto a ship is more than transportation—it’s transformation.

Would I cruise again? Without question. That first voyage left me hungry for more: longer itineraries, new destinations, bigger ships. But no matter how many sailings come after, none will ever quite match the thrill of that first horn blast, that first sail-away, that first step into the vast unknown of the sea.
