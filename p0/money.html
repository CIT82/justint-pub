Money & Extras: What I Spent (and What I Shouldn’t Have)

One of the biggest surprises of my first Carnival cruise was how easy it was to spend money without realizing it. On land, I’m used to swiping a card and watching the balance drop. At sea, everything runs through your Sail & Sign account. That little plastic card unlocks your cabin, buys your drinks, and settles the bill in the casino. It’s convenient — almost too convenient. By the end of day one, I had already learned a few lessons about cruise money management the hard way.

The Sail & Sign Card: My Floating Wallet

Carnival makes it simple: link a credit card (or deposit cash), then use your Sail & Sign card everywhere onboard. No cash, no coins, no tipping at every turn. At first, it felt liberating. I grabbed a coffee, picked up a T-shirt in the gift shop, ordered a piña colada by the pool. Each time, I just tapped the card and walked away. It wasn’t until I checked the balance on the Hub app that I realized those $5 and $10 charges added up faster than waves hitting the bow.

Lesson learned: check your balance daily. It’s easier to course-correct mid-sailing than to face a shock at disembarkation.

Drink Packages: Worth It or Not?

Before sailing, I debated the CHEERS! drink package. Unlimited soda, specialty coffees, cocktails — it sounded tempting. At around $60 per day, it wasn’t cheap. I decided to skip it and pay à la carte, thinking I’d save money. By day three, after a couple of frozen cocktails, evening wines, and lattes from JavaBlue, I realized I had spent just as much as the package would have cost.

My takeaway: if you like having more than two or three drinks a day (including sodas and specialty coffees), the package pays for itself. If you’re a light drinker, stick to water, iced tea, and lemonade — all included in your fare.

Specialty Dining: Splurges That Felt Worth It

The main dining room and buffet are included, and honestly, they’re more than enough. But I did splurge on a night at Fahrenheit 555, Carnival’s steakhouse. For a fixed price, I had filet mignon, lobster bisque, and a dessert so rich it practically glowed. The service was elevated, the pace more relaxed, and it felt like a date night at sea.

I also tried Cucina del Capitano, the Italian specialty venue, where pasta was made fresh and portions generous. Both experiences were memorable, and the upcharge felt justified — but I wouldn’t recommend booking every night. Pick one or two for a splurge, enjoy the variety, and save the rest of your budget for adventures ashore.

Gratuities: Plan Ahead

Gratuities aren’t optional. Carnival automatically adds them to your account (around $16 per person, per day). That covers your stateroom steward, dining staff, and behind-the-scenes crew who keep the ship running. You can prepay before sailing, which I recommend — it smooths out the budget and means no surprise charges at the end.

I also set aside a little cash for extra tipping. My cabin steward went above and beyond, so sliding him a $20 on the last night felt right. Same for the bartender who remembered my order without asking.

Hidden Costs and Easy Wins

Beyond drinks and dining, I found small extras that quietly added up: photos snapped by ship photographers, arcade credits, a round in the casino, duty-free shopping. None were outrageous, but together they nudged the bill upward.

On the flip side, some of the best things on board were free: comedy shows, live music, trivia, pool games, movies on the deck. I learned to balance the splurges with the freebies, which made the cruise feel richer without making my wallet lighter.

Port Day Spending

Don’t forget the budget doesn’t stop at the gangway. In Ensenada, I dropped cash on tacos, souvenirs, and a spur-of-the-moment horseback ride on the beach. Shore excursions booked through Carnival cost more but came with peace of mind — the ship waits if their tour runs late. Independent excursions were cheaper, but I made sure to keep ship time on my watch so I didn’t become a pier-runner.

Bottom Line: Cruise Cash Lessons

At the end of the trip, my Sail & Sign statement wasn’t shocking, but it was a wake-up call. Cruising makes it easy to spend without noticing, and the extras can rival the base fare if you’re not mindful. My strategy for the next cruise is simple: prepay gratuities, budget daily for drinks and extras, and say yes to one or two splurges that feel special.

Because the truth is, the memories aren’t in the receipts. They’re in the laughter at the comedy club, the steak dinner that felt like an anniversary, and the surprise moment when a stranger becomes a friend over a round of piña coladas. Money comes and goes — but the cruise experience is priceless.
